The Lomography Holga is not a good camera by any objective measure. The body is plastic, the lens is plastic, it leaks light, using it is total guesswork, and no two are alike. I have a very high hit rate with my Holga. Its objective imperfections produce subjective qualities that can't really be replicated.

<HolgaIllustration />

I started with digital. I was close to some now-famous people and would document their small and sweaty shows with an aim to capture the energy and motion by mixing long exposures and flash to get clear and recognisable faces among random blur and light. While I really liked the results, the need to use flash close up to a performer was not particularly popular.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20070414-221349" />

I started to reduce the polish even further, and where there was less light to play with I'd fake double exposure with the flash. This look of error and abstraction was really hard to apply in other settings, and with fewer and fewer opportunities to shoot music I was looking for something else.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20070125-221414" />

[Pi](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(film)>), Darren Aronofsky's 1998 film, was a big deal for me. Deliberately confrontational; high contrast, low resolution, grainy black and white. I wanted to get that feeling in my photographs.

I learned of the Holga from <span lang="fr">[Mes Vacances Avec Holga](https://www.printedmatter.org/catalog/13975/)</span> by Frederic
Lebain, a collection of photos that lean more into dreamy abstraction than dark isolation. I found it
at the [Photographers' Gallery](https://thephotographersgallery.org.uk/) and immediately ordered the camera
from Lomography. It was around £30, and film was so cheap and abundant it was a much more sensible choice than anything digital. The Nikon was sold to pay bills, and the Holga became my everyday carry.

It has one shutter speed of `1/100`, an aperture of `f8` or `f11` (labelled as 'sunny' and 'cloudy') and can be focussed from 'mountains' to 'one person'. For my particular camera, focussing made no visible difference and I always shot at 'sunny' to avoid underexposure.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20260404-185629" />

With two sides of the exposure triangle eliminated film choice was the only real variable under my control. I got pretty good at matching speed and colour to the situation and taking the known quirks of the camera into account. Fast or slow film, one making grain and contrast more prominent and the other looking smoother but being more prone to light leaks.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20260401-145046" />

It's very common for Holga owners to tape the camera to help reduce light leaks. My modifications went a little further; removing the battery terminals for the flash, removing the mask between the shutter and film, taping over the flash and the switch for aspect ratio, and filling in any white printed logos with a sharpie. All of this helps with reliability and makes the camera disappear when held against my chest.

I got tired of the Holga aesthetic after a couple of years and moved on to other medium format film cameras. Ironically, I got closest to the Pi aesthetic with the most expensive film camera I ever owned. With fast film and fine control through a lens actually made of glass, it offered more deliberate choices with fewer side effects. Just in time for film prices and a desire to [scan all of my film](/writing/my-film-scanning-process) to tip me back to digital.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20260311-204028" />

The Holga has zero monetary value, but due to its particular characteristics it is, in a way, the most precious camera I have owned. The extreme quality control of high end and modern cameras and lenses makes them pretty much interchangeable. I had a second Holga for a while, a 120TLR that I lent to someone never to be seen again. The body was far more light-tight, it had a huge waist-level finder, and a bulb setting. Objectively better, but not quite the same.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20260408-190521" />

Any desire to use it again is probably just nostalgia. I don't think it's the style I want anymore, it's that feeling of freedom with the camera and being excited with the results. The closest I got to that with a digital camera was my brief ownership of a [Leica M-D](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leica_M-D_(Typ_262)>). As simple as it gets but (ironically again) I didn't like the degradation in low light.

<InlinePhoto photoId="20260404-185208" />

The Holga is going to stay with me as a museum piece. A reflection of the time and place where I used it, but not something I'm likely to revisit. There are many like it, but this one is mine. Irreplaceable and barely usable.
