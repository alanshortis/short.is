The Lomography Holga is not a good camera by any objective measure. The body is plastic, then lens is plastic, it leaks light, it might pop open in your bag, using it is total guesswork, and no two are alike.

What makes a photograph good is subjective, and I have a very high hit rate with my Holga. These objective imperfections make for subjective qualities that cannot be replicated.

<aside>There are some photography clichés in here</aside>

My first steps into photography were digital, with a pretty nice Sony. I would point it at things, take the picture, and not really consider what I was doing. This wasn't for any reason other than a lack of understanding of the huge number of settings and options. I studied the camera just enough to get some pretty good results, but it never really clicked for me. Everything was very clean.

Next, a better digital camera and a better understanding of how to take pictures. At the time I was close with some now-famous people and took the time to document tiny, sweaty shows. I wanted to capture the motion and energy, and I did that with long exposures and a flash, getting a mixture of motion blur with sharp, recognisable faces. Deliberate degradation and random imperfaction. I loved it, but the people I was shooting did not; flash in their eyes during the show, and overly abstract images after.

[Pi](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(film)>), a 1998 film by Darren Aronofsky was a big deal for me around this time. Deliberately confrontational, high contrast low resolution film. The film would be completely different if shot in clean colour. I wanted to make photos like that.

How I ended up with the Holga is a little hazy. I am fairly sure I found a copy of <span lang="fr">[Mes Vacances Avec Holga](https://www.printedmatter.org/catalog/13975/)</span> at the [Photographers Gallery](https://thephotographersgallery.org.uk/), loved what I saw, then got the camera from Lomography directly. What I know for certain is that it must have been very cheap, because I could afford it and my digital cameras had already been sold to pay bills.

Film choice is the only variable one can truly control with a Holga, and film was cheap and abundant. Supposedly the camera does have settings; the lens turns to focus, and there is a switch that toggles between 'sunny' and 'cloudy'. Neother of these had any affect. Grand Central Station is pretty dark: `3200`. Coney Island on a sunny day: `100`. Beyond that, I never thought about what I was taking, never looked through the viewfinder, and just responded to what I saw as quickly as possible.
