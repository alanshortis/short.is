Getting film photographs into a digital format can be as complex as you want to make it. There are scanners, there is hardware to shoot the film with a digital camera, and (for the most sane among us) labs that will do it immediately after processing. I have tried all three, and I have landed firmly on camera scanning for the best results.

## The right tools for the job

If you don't have the right tool for the job, the next best tool is the one you already have. I recently sold my Leica M10 Monochrom and replaced it with an M10-P. I did not want to sell the Monochrom, but as I can't really justify even one high-end camera I had to switch to one camera that I can shoot and scan with.

If you know anything about a Leica M, you're probably questioning my choices right now. You'll know that they're deliberately manual and deliberately slow to use, but none of this is a particular drawback for scanning. I need to remove it from the copy stand every time I want to get to the latest batch of scans, as the SD card is under the removable base plate and it is not possible to tether. The app connection is so spotty that I can't really control it remotely. The screen has no articulation. It's all a little bit awkward, but all liveable for the few hundred frames I have to scan.

After researching the small number of viable lens options I found a Leica Macro Elmarit-R 60mm f2.8, added an URTH adapter, and a macro extension tube. This stack of parts enables true 1:1 reproduction for 35mm film on the full-frame sensor through Leica glass for just a few hundred pounds.

The camera is mounted to a Negative Supply copy stand, looking down on their 'enthusiast' film carrier and light source. These are 3D printed, pretty fiddly to use, and not particularly well made relative to the higher-end options.

## Camera settings

The M10 is easy to set up for scanning. I switch off what little automation there is, set 100 ISO (the M10's minimum), set the lens to f/8, switch off exposure compensation, and set exposure to cater for either the whole roll or the individual frame.

There is a reason for the variety of approaches to shutter speed. For a full roll of well-exposed film, I use the histogram to set exposure just under the point of blowing out the highlights on the most dense frame.

Probably my best work was captured using a Holga 120SF. Some of the details of these photos are barely visible on the negative, but they're in there. In these cases, I still use the histogram but I do it for every frame, and even then it's still hard to see anything until it's been converted to a positive image. I still struggle with everything being the opposite of what's expected when scanning; blown highlights are lost shadows.

## Conversion

Unlike a scanner, a camera has no option to invert a negative image automatically. I am not smart enough to understand the various algorithms used to do this, but I do know that a simple inversion in Lightroom or Photoshop isn't likely to get the best results. I use [Negative Lab Pro](https://www.negativelabpro.com/) in exactly the way they suggest:

1. If it's a colour image, set white balance from the mask around the film
2. <kbd>Ctrl</kbd> + <kbd>n</kbd> to open NLP
3. I like the 'Frontier' colour model, or 'B+W'. Hit 'convert'
4. Adjust the image as necessary

I don't do a lot of post-processing; it's usually limited to balancing contrast, highlights, and shadows. A little dust removal, walk away for a while, come back to it and make sure it looks right.

## From Lightroom to the website

To put as few barriers as possible between a scan and publication, I have a bash script that uses ImageMagick to create three avif variants of each export from Lightroom (natural size, 2x, and 4x to cover high pixel densities) before uploading them to an S3 bucket. Sizing is slightly adjusted to the aspect ratio of the image so the many square photos aren't too large. I use a bucket to get the benefits of a CDN and to keep images out of my repository and hosting.

Once uploaded, I manually add an entry to a [collection in Astro](https://docs.astro.build/en/guides/content-collections/) so it knows where to look. While I probably could automate this step, I prefer to keep it manual so I can write `alt` text and ensure the images are ordered in a way that looks right on the page.

Despite the manual step, the level of automation is high enough to get a photograph scanned and published in just a few minutes.

## Camera vs scanner

I digitised everything a few years ago using the cheapest Epson photo scanner available. It could handle 35mm (badly), medium format (better), and do it all very slowly. There was very little control over exposure, and the feedback loop was extremely long, making me very unlikely to do multiple passes.

There are a few photos I believed had no detail at all until I scanned them with a camera. While the aesthetic of a lot of my work with Holga deliberately leans into grain, darkness, and imperfections, the scanner hid details that made some photos worth another look.

## Future scans

This whole process has been a means to digitise film from the last 20 years or so in a way that means it's very unlikely I'll do it again. I don't really shoot film anymore, but never saying never means I'll keep the equipment as it is. A roll of film from time to time does not warrant improvement though I am one lottery win away from a [100MP Hasselblad](https://www.hasselblad.com/x-system/x2d-ii-100c/).
