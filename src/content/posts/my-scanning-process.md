Getting film photographs into a digital format is more complex than it seems like it should be. There are scanners, there is hardware to shoot the film with a digital camera, and (for the most sane among us) labs that will do it immediately after processing. I have tried all three, and this is where I have ended up.

## The right tools for the job

If you don't have right tool for the job, the next best tool is the one you already have. I recently sold my Leica M10 Monochrom and replaced it with a M10-P. I did not want to sell the Monochrom, but as I can't really afford to own even one high end camera it wasn't feasible to get something exclusively for scanning.

If you know anything about a Leica M, you'll know that they're deliberately manual and deliberately slow to use. None of this is a particular drawback for scanning; everything is slow and controlled anyway.

The main problem with the Leica is the need to remove it from the copy stand every time I want to get to the latest batch of scans, as the SD card is under the removable base plate and it is not possible to tether. Otherwise, live view is good, it has focus peaking, and there is a histogram to check exposure.

I didn't even look for a native M-mount macro lens; it would have either been prohibitively expensive or an unproven Chinese clone. After a little research I found Leica R Macro lens and added an URTH R-M adapter, and a Macro extension tube. This hodgepodge of parts enables true 1:1 reproduction for 35mm film on the M10's full frame sensor through Leica glass. The bulk of my film is medium format, so I usually don't need the macro extension tube.

The camera and lens combo are mounted to a negative supply copy stand, looking down on their 'enthusiast' film carrier and and light source. These are 3D printed, pretty fiddly to use, and not particularly well made relative to the higher end options but I only have a few hundred negatives to scan and am unlikely to shoot film very often.

## Camera settings

I don't do anything particularly special here. I switch off all automation, manually set the camera to 100ISO (the M10's minimum), set the lens to f8 (a few trial shots showed this to be the sharpest, but it's negligible between f8 and f5.6), switch off exposure compensation, and set exposure to cater for either the whole roll or the individual frame.

There is a reason for the variety of approaches to shutter speed. For a full roll of well exposed film, I use the histogram to set exposure just under the point of blowing out the highlights. This is pretty consistent, but absolutely unusable for the majority of my photographs.

Probably my best work was captured using a Holga 120SF. Some of the details of these photos are barely visible on the negative, but it's there. In these cases, I still use the histogram but I do it for every frame.
