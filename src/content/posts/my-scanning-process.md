Getting film photographs into a digital format is more complex than it seems like it should be. There are scanners, there is hardware to shoot the film with a digital camera, and (for the most sane among us) labs that will do it immediately after processing. I have tried all three, and this is where I have ended up.

## The right tool for the job

If you don't have right tool for the job, the next best tool is the one you already have. I recently sold my Leica M10 Monochrom and replaced it with a M10-P. I did not want to sell the Monochrom, but as I can't really afford to own one high end camera it wasn't feasible to buy something exclusively for scanning.

If you know anything about a Leica M, you'll know that they're deliberately manual and deliberately slow to use. None of this is a particular drawback for scanning; I am not trying to catch focus for a split second and I am not dealing with difficult light. Everything is still slow, and controlled.

### Lens

Lenses native to Leica M bodies are very limited, very expensive, or both. My best bet was to adapt something else and live with it being decoupled from the camera's rangefinder, and I was able to find a low priced Leica R Macro lens, an URTH R-M adapter, and a genuine Macro extension tube. This hodge podge of parts enables true 1:1 reproduction for 35mm film on the M10's full frame sensor.
